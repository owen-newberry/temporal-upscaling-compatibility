Introduction
Over the past two decades, video games have evolved from relatively simple interactive experiences into complex, real-time systems that rival film, simulation software, and scientific visualization in both technical sophistication and cultural influence. Modern games are no longer defined solely by their artistic design or narrative ambition, but by the hardware architectures that make those ambitions technically possible. Among the most influential of these technologies are graphics processing units (GPUs), which have transitioned from specialized rendering hardware into general-purpose, highly parallel computing platforms capable of supporting artificial intelligence, physics simulation, and real-time data processing.
Recent advances in GPU design, particularly Nvidia’s increasing emphasis on AI-focused architectures, represent a fundamental shift in how games are developed. Features such as real-time ray tracing, neural upscaling, frame generation, and AI-assisted animation systems are no longer experimental add-ons; they are becoming core assumptions in modern game engines. As a result, software engineers and technical designers are being pushed to rethink long-standing development workflows, optimization strategies, and architectural design patterns that were originally built around more traditional rendering pipelines.
This project investigates how modern GPU architectures, with a particular focus on Nvidia’s recent Blackwell architecture, are reshaping the principles and practices of software engineering in video game development. Rather than treating hardware as a passive constraint, this research approaches GPUs as active drivers of software design, influencing how developers structure code, distribute computation, and balance visual fidelity with performance. By examining both industry documentation and real-world development tools, this project aims to bridge the gap between hardware innovation and software methodology.
This topic directly connects to my degree in Applied Software Engineering and my career interest in game and interactive media development. As game engines increasingly abstract low-level rendering details, software engineers must understand the architectural assumptions embedded within these tools in order to design scalable, maintainable, and future-proof systems. Understanding how AI-integrated GPUs influence engine design, rendering pipelines, and optimization strategies is therefore critical not only for game developers, but for anyone working at the intersection of software engineering and high-performance computing.

Intended Scholarly Question
How have recent advancements in computer hardware, particularly GPUs and their integration of AI-focused architectures, reshaped the software engineering principles, design patterns, and development workflows of modern video games?

Preliminary Research and/or Literature Review
For computer enthusiasts, Nvidia is a household name, and for good reason. Nvidia has been, and continues to be, a leading innovator in the graphics card realm, being the most popular brand for consumers and businesses alike. RTX graphics cards (Nvidia’s consumer line of graphics cards) are omnipresent in custom and pre-built computers around the world, especially for performance builds.
Nvidia released their new RTX 50 series graphics cards on January 30th, 2025. Obtaining these cards has proven difficult, and their full industry impact is yet to be seen. The new cards feature the new Blackwell Architecture and are notable for several reasons: Nvidia promises that these new graphics cards will produce up to double the performance speed of their last-generation desktop equivalent, improve laptop performance up to eight times faster than the last generation, and possibly most notably, designate much more of the chip for AI and neural rendering processes. These enhancements make Blackwell GPUs a fundamental change in both gaming and professional applications of the component.
Advances in GPU technology, which affects how programmers design and optimize code for new hardware fueled development of software, games, and media. Nvidia plans to use the Blackwell Architecture for more than just consumer activities like gaming or productivity. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang called the Blackwell “a processor for the generative AI era,” which could potentially revolutionize the development of AI large-language models like ChatGPT.
Large language models require incredible amounts of power (and subsequently energy) to compute in real time. The demanding nature of training AI models means that organizations are confronted with a need for memory and servers, which Nvidia says the “Blackwell architecture and portfolio of products are designed to address the needs of ever-increasing AI model sizes and parameters with a long list of new innovations.”
To understand the significance of the Blackwell architecture, it is helpful to look at how Nvidia’s prior graphics card architectures and how they have changed over time. Prior graphics cards were made to render visuals and visual effects for applications in real time, but they also served purposes in data processing and scientific computing. In addition to playing a larger role, particularly in domains like artificial intelligence (AI), these modern graphics cards feature complete chip components specifically built for AI. This change has been led by Nvidia, which can handle both new computing demands and more conventional graphics workloads.
The Ada Lovelace architecture was introduced with the RTX 40 series of graphics cards, the now previous generation of Nvidia’s graphics cards. The RTX 40 series brought significant advancements in ray tracing, a rendering technique that simulates how light interacts with objects. 
Ray tracing might be most known by its fanatics in the gaming world, but it is arguably more used in areas like movie and media production because of its ability to create more realistic shadows, reflections, and lighting effects. Ray tracing has historically been seen as extremely computationally demanding, but that is starting to change following improvements around the Ada Lovelace and Blackwell architectures. Rasterization, the process of turning 2D polygons into 3D images, then mapping out each individual pixel on a 2D screen to display an image, is the predecessor to ray tracing. It is important to note that the process of rasterization will always be less intensive on the graphics card because of the nature of the process, but the cost of rasterization is the image quality and feel. Especially for media creation, ray tracing will always be preferred because of realistic looking visuals. While big-budget movie studios have lots of funding to run supercomputers that can handle rendering in stark detail, Blackwell architecture chips will bring an improved level of performance to consumer level ray tracing. With the improvements in power and efficiency made by Nvidia, ray tracing could start to be within reach of the average gamer, and at a level we haven't seen before. Some new games could even require ray tracing to run, like the recently released Indiana Jones and the Great Circle.
With the introduction of the Blackwell architecture, Nvidia is taking a more dramatic step forward, particularly in its focus on AI-driven computing. While previous architectures, including Ada Lovelace, incorporated AI-based features as supplementary tools—such as using AI to enhance image resolution and improve frame rates (DLSS) — Blackwell integrates AI as a core part of GPU functionality. This means that instead of AI being just an optional enhancement for graphics, Nvidia is designing these new GPUs with AI workloads in mind from the ground up. AI workloads, such as training large language models, running real-time neural network inference, and processing vast amounts of data, require specialized hardware to run efficiently. Blackwell introduces improvements in dedicated AI processing units, memory bandwidth, and energy efficiency to handle these demands. This shift reflects a broader industry trend where GPUs are no longer just for gaming but are becoming indispensable for AI research, content creation, and high-performance computing in fields ranging from medicine to autonomous driving.

From a high abstraction viewpoint, I want to look at documentation and literature analysis of tools and hardware shaping game development, case studies of recent games and engines, qualitative synthesis of developer commentary and experiences, a degree of an experiential and observational component, and a comprehensive interpretation.
Documentation and Literature Analysis
This is two-fold: analyzing and establishing a historical and technical foundation of how game development has existed and changed with previous hardware and software capability advancements and looking forward to current hardware and software changes that are shaping game development currently.
This includes surveying academic and industry literature for already documented effects of changes with the development process, along with, identifying architectural inflection points (breakthroughs in hardware technology like SSDs, DDR5, etc), and extracting recurring engineering patterns.

Significance
This project is significant both academically and professionally. For software engineering as a discipline, it addresses an underexplored area where hardware innovation directly influences software design patterns, challenging the assumption that abstraction layers fully insulate developers from underlying architecture. By analyzing how AI-driven GPU features reshape development workflows, this research contributes to broader discussions on co-design between hardware and software systems.
On a professional level, this project directly informs my career goals in game and interactive media development. As modern engines increasingly rely on AI-assisted rendering and parallel computation, developers who understand these systems at an architectural level are better equipped to design efficient, scalable, and maintainable software. This research provides a foundation for engaging critically with emerging tools rather than treating them as opaque black boxes.
Beyond academia and industry, the findings may also be relevant to broader conversations about accessibility, performance expectations, and sustainability in gaming. As cutting-edge features become hardware requirements rather than optional enhancements, understanding their implications becomes essential for developers, players, and researchers alike.
